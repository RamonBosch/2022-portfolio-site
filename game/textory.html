Textory

SUMMARY

Textory is my first attempt at game design, a simple game aimed at mobile devices, developed in two months with no previous experience in coding or 3D modeling.

I wanted to work on something simple and minimal to learn as much as possible. Developing a third person, isometric maze game seemed feasible, and would work fine on a mobile device.

First ideas

When thinking about ways of making a maze game, I considered using a main character that could walk through walls, so if the player got stuck, there may always be an alternative way out.

This ability would only be possible to use a certain number of times, and may need to be charged to be available again. The maze would have enemies, the player would have to run from them and find healing items when low on HP.

A final fun element I thought I might be able to implement would be making the maze move or change, to make it more varied, challenging, even replayable.

Game breakdown

In Textory the player controls Sendy, a text messenger that travels through the internet to deliver text messages from one device to another.

Tutorial

We begin the game inside a cellphone. Here Softie (the program's software) explains that Sendy has to deliver a text to another device located on the other side of town. The blue box walks us through the tutorial, so we can learn about Sendy's abilities.

Battery and signal

Sendy can send itself to any point visible within the limits of our mobile screen, but only five times, then our signal is out.

To charge the signal, there are Wi-Fi hotspots that fill up our Wi-Fi bars. We can also send our character back to the last visited Wi-Fi hotspot free of charge.

Battery equals health points in Textory and is drained when we're hit by malware agents such as Blocky or Bouncy. Ten hits and Sendy is knocked out, having to restart the level.

To advance through the levels we have to collect keys and cross big ramps quickly to finally arrive at our goal and deliver the text.

Visuals

For Textory, I chose to mimic late 80s videogame art aesthetics, like illustrations found in old game cartridges. These old game illustrations ususally depict technology in a very fantastic way, humanizing digital elements or products in creative, even misleading ways.

This aesthetic felt fitting with a game like Textory, in which sending a text message becomes an epic journey. The logo is shaped like a speech bubble, and is also inspired by retro game logos such as Balloon Fighter, Dig Dug or Popeye.

The user interface is simple, a joystick lets the player move Sendy and a button can be used to get back to the last hotspot visited. Its elements use a color code that is consistent with the rest of the game: turquoise for wi-fi signal, green for battery, yellow for text messages and coral for ability buttons.

The main menu apes a texting app, as the player makes choices, new text bubbles appear at the bottom of the screen and go up until they overflow the screen.

Sendy

Sendy is the abstract personification of a text message.

Design

The final design is vaguely inspired by the original Sonic The Hedgehog. I wanted to convey a sense of dynamism and speed while keeping the design as minimal as possible.

The shape of Sendy's head is like the speech bubbles often used in text apps.

Animations

The disjointed parts that form Sendy's body can be stretched and rearranged to easily express velocity, rest or pain.

Idle: Sendy is always hovering with its body pulsating.

Flight: when Sendy sends itself from one point to another, it momentarily disappears from the screen to land somewhere else.

Flinch: when malware damages Sendy it will flinch slightly.

Knock out: if Sendy runs out of battery, its body will fall backwards and into pieces.

NPCs

I couldn't work on the NPCs until late in the development of the game, when the latest version became stable and I had enough knowledge to apply some very rudimentary automated behaviour.

Blocky

Bright, red, with dark sunglasses, it was designed to convey heaviness. Blockies clog the path, but make slow jumps from time to time that let Sendy slip underneath and keep advancing.

Bouncy

Also bright, red, with dark sunglasses, Bouncies make ample, quick jumps and may bump into a careless Sendy from time to time. They usually wander on spinning platforms, which makes them faster and harder to avoid.

Softie

The blue one with the spectacles and soft edges. Softie is the personification of software and will assist Sendy during the tutorial to teach the player how to play the game.

Items and background

I added them at the very end of the development process, once the game was practically finished and functioning properly.

Items

All three designs are a combination of a real life object (their function in the game), with a common icon from a mobile interface (their appearance in the Textory world).

The text message looks both like a speech bubble and a letter, the battery healer looks like a AA battery and a smoothie, and the signal healer looks like a cone of fries and three wi-fi bars.

Background

I designed 90 different buildings and turned them into a repeating pattern to depict a busy background city.

In the final game, I also added a few moving, oval-shaped clouds to accentuate the sense of depth and height.

Gameplay

This is a gameplay recording of the final version of the game.

The app performed correctly on mobile devices and the framerate remained stable for the whole duration of the demo. The controls weren't very precise at times and the design of the level was very rough. If I had proceeded with the project's development, I would have taken care of solving those two problems at first.


In conclusion, I'm happy I managed to develop a game this far in the span of only two months. Not only did I become more proficient in developing with Unreal Engine 4 but also modeling with Blender, another new tool for me at the time. I learned about the limitations of developing for mobile devices and was able to deal with basic development mistakes in a risk-free environment. Most of the lessons I learned by developing Textory became useful when I worked on my next game, and so I inted to keep getting better and better with every new game I make.
